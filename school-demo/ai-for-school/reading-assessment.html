Bloom's Taxonomy for Reading Comprehension

Effective reading assessments span all cognitive levels. Use this framework to ensure your questions build from basic recall to critical analysis:

1. REMEMBER — Recall facts and basic concepts
Keywords: Define, identify, list, name, recall
Example: "Who is the protagonist of the story?"
2. UNDERSTAND — Explain ideas or concepts
Keywords: Classify, describe, explain, paraphrase, summarize
Example: "In your own words, what happened in Chapter 3?"
3. APPLY — Use information in new situations
Keywords: Demonstrate, illustrate, interpret, use, apply
Example: "How would the protagonist react in this new situation?"
4. ANALYZE — Draw connections among ideas
Keywords: Compare, contrast, distinguish, examine, analyze
Example: "What is the relationship between the setting and the character's mood?"
5. EVALUATE — Justify a stand or decision
Keywords: Appraise, defend, judge, support, evaluate
Example: "Was the protagonist's decision justified? Support your answer with evidence."
6. CREATE — Produce new or original work
Keywords: Design, develop, formulate, compose, construct
Example: "Write an alternative ending that changes the theme of the story."

AI Prompts for Question Generation

Prompt 1: Balanced Question Set

🎯 Copy This Prompt

Generate 12 reading comprehension questions for [TEXT] at [GRADE LEVEL].

Distribute across Bloom's Taxonomy:
- 2 Remember (recall details)
- 2 Understand (explain concepts)
- 2 Apply (make connections)
- 3 Analyze (examine relationships)
- 2 Evaluate (make judgments)
- 1 Create (produce something new)

For each question:
- Include the specific text evidence needed to answer
- Note the cognitive level
- Provide a model answer

Prompt 2: Differentiated by Proficiency

🎯 Copy This Prompt

Create 3 versions of comprehension questions for [TEXT]:

VERSION A (Emerging EAL):
- 8 questions total
- Focus: Literal comprehension, vocabulary in context
- Formats: Multiple choice, matching, true/false with text evidence
- Sentence frames provided for written responses

VERSION B (Developing EAL):
- 10 questions total
- Focus: Inferential understanding, character motivation
- Formats: Short answer, fill-in, simple graphic organizers
- Word bank for written responses

VERSION C (Expanding EAL):
- 12 questions total
- Focus: Critical analysis, theme development, author's craft
- Formats: Extended response, essay, Socratic questions
- Open-ended with multiple valid interpretations

Prompt 3: Text-Dependent Questions

🎯 Copy This Prompt

Generate text-dependent questions for these specific passages from [TEXT]:

[PROVIDE 3-5 KEY QUOTES OR PASSAGES]

For each passage:
1. Literal question (what does it say?)
2. Inferential question (what does it imply?)
3. Analytical question (why does it matter?)
4. Comparative question (how does it connect to other parts?)

All answers must be supported with direct text evidence.

Complete Question Bank Examples

🎭 Sample: "Romeo and Juliet" Act 2 Scene 2 (Balcony Scene)

1. What time of day does this scene take place? Remember
2. Paraphrase Juliet's "What's in a name?" speech in modern English. Understand
3. How might Romeo's words sound different if he knew Juliet was listening? Apply
4. Compare Romeo's language when he thinks he's alone versus when he's speaking to Juliet. What changes? Analyze
5. Is love at first sight possible, based on Romeo and Juliet's interaction? Use evidence to support your position. Evaluate
6. Write a soliloquy from the Nurse's perspective observing this scene. What would she think and feel? Create

Assessment Types for Reading Comprehension

📝 Traditional Comprehension Quiz

Best for: Quick check for understanding, plot recall, basic comprehension

Format: 10-15 questions mixing MC, short answer, matching

AI Prompt: "Create a 15-question reading check quiz for Chapters 1-3 of [BOOK]. Include 8 MC, 4 short answer (1-2 sentences), 3 matching. Focus on plot, characters, and key events. Include answer key."

🎭 Performance-Based Assessment

Best for: Deep comprehension, multiple interpretations, creative engagement

Format: Students choose from 3-4 options (dramatic reading, artistic response, written analysis, multimedia)

AI Prompt: "Design a performance-based assessment for [TEXT] with 4 options: (1) Dramatic monologue from a minor character's perspective, (2) Visual art piece with artist statement, (3) Analytical essay on theme, (4) Video 'book trailer.' Include rubric for each option."

🔍 Document-Based Analysis (DBA)

Best for: Close reading, evidence-based argumentation, literary analysis

Format: Students analyze 3-5 key passages and write a structured response

AI Prompt: "Create a Document-Based Analysis for [TEXT] with 4 excerpts. For each excerpt: provide the text, 2 scaffolding questions, and space for annotation. Final prompt: 'Using at least 3 excerpts, analyze how the author develops [THEME/CHARACTER].'"

💬 Socratic Seminar Assessment

Best for: Discussion skills, critical thinking, listening comprehension

Format: Student-led discussion with prepared questions and participation rubric

AI Prompt: "Generate 8 open-ended Socratic questions for [TEXT]. Questions should have no single right answer, require text evidence, and spark debate. Include preparation guide for students and participation rubric."

📊 Reading Portfolio

Best for: Growth over time, self-reflection, metacognition

Format: Collection of reading responses, annotations, reflections throughout a unit

AI Prompt: "Design a reading portfolio assignment for [TEXT/UNIT]. Include: 5 different response formats (journal entry, character map, theme tracker, quote analysis, creative piece), reflection prompts, and portfolio rubric."

Reading Assessment Rubrics

Literary Analysis Rubric (Single-Point Format)
Criteria Concerns (Below) Proficient (Target) Advanced (Above)
Claim/Thesis Unclear or missing claim about the text Clear, arguable claim about literary element with preview of reasoning Sophisticated claim showing nuanced understanding of text complexity
Evidence Evidence unrelated or insufficient to support claim Relevant textual evidence with proper citations Well-selected evidence including literary devices and techniques
Analysis Summary without interpretation Explains HOW evidence supports claim; shows reasoning Insightful analysis revealing deeper layers of meaning
Organization Confusing structure; no transitions Logical structure with clear transitions and conclusion Sophisticated organization enhancing argument flow
Conventions Errors interfere with meaning Few errors; doesn't interfere with communication Consistent control; appropriate academic register

For each criterion, provide:
- Concerns column (common errors, missing elements)
- Proficient column (target performance)

Consider EAL learners: highlight where sentence frames or vocabulary support would help.

Formative Assessment Strategies

Use these low-stakes checks throughout your unit to guide instruction:

  • Exit Tickets (3-5 minutes): "One thing I learned... One thing I'm confused about... One question I have..."
  • Thumbs Up/Down: Quick comprehension check on key plot points or vocabulary
  • One-Sentence Summary: Students summarize a chapter in exactly 20 words
  • Quote Response: Project a key quote; students write immediate reaction or question
  • Character Hot Seat: Student answers questions in character (assesses deep comprehension)
  • Predict-Observe-Explain: Predict what happens next, read, explain prediction accuracy
🎯 The Feedback Loop:

Assessment without feedback is just grading. Build in time for:
1. Students self-assess using the rubric
2. Peer feedback using specific protocols
3. Teacher feedback on process, not just product
4. Revision opportunities based on feedback
