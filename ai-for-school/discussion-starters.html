Why Discussion Matters for EAL Students

Discussion is where language acquisition meets critical thinking. For EAL learners, structured talk:

  • Lowers the affective filter: Less pressure than writing; mistakes are temporary
  • Provides processing time: Students hear peer ideas before formulating their own
  • Builds academic vocabulary: "I agree with..." "I'd like to add..." "A counterexample is..."
  • Validates multiple perspectives: No single "right" answer in interpretation

The Question Type Toolkit

1. Interpretive Questions (No Single Right Answer)

Purpose: Generate debate; multiple valid interpretations supported by text

"Is [character] truly [adjective], or do their actions suggest something else?"
"What is the author's message about [theme]? Is it optimistic or pessimistic?"
"Who is most responsible for [event]? Defend your position."

2. Comparative Questions (Text-to-Text, Self, World)

Purpose: Build connections; make abstract themes concrete

"How does [character's] situation compare to [current event/historical situation]?"
"Have you ever faced a choice like [character]? What did you do?"
"How would this story be different if set in [different time/place/culture]?"

3. Evaluative Questions (Judgment & Criteria)

Purpose: Develop critical thinking; establish and defend criteria

"Was [character's] decision justified? What criteria should we use to judge?"
"Is [character] a hero, a villain, or something else entirely?"
"Does the ending satisfy you? Why or why not?"

4. Speculative Questions (Hypotheticals)

Purpose: Explore causality; understand character motivation

"What would happen if [character] made a different choice at [moment]?"
"If you could give [character] one piece of advice, what would it be?"
"How might the story change if told from [different character's] perspective?"

5. Authorial Choice Questions (Craft & Technique)

Purpose: Analyze literary devices; understand author's purpose

"Why do you think the author chose to [specific technique]? What effect does it create?"
"What is the significance of [symbol/motif] appearing at this moment?"
"How does the setting reflect the characters' internal states?"

6. Universal Theme Questions (Big Ideas)

Purpose: Connect literature to life; enduring human questions

"What does this story teach us about the nature of [love/power/justice/freedom]?"
"Is [theme] portrayed as positive or destructive in this text?"
"How does this story challenge or confirm our assumptions about [human nature/society]?"

Prompt 1: Full Socratic Seminar Question Set

Provide:
- 4 core questions for inner circle

- Switching protocol (when and how)

Discussion Protocols That Work

🔄 Think-Pair-Share

Best for: Warm-up; EAL students who need processing time

  1. Think (1 min): Individual reflection on question
  2. Pair (3 min): Share with partner; refine thinking
  3. Share (5 min): Pairs report to whole class

Sentence starters for pairs: "I think... because..." / "I see it differently..." / "Can you explain why...?"

🔄 Concentric Circles (Inside-Outside)

Best for: Rapid idea exchange; practicing concise expression

  1. Form two circles: inner facing out, outer facing in
  2. Each pair discusses question for 2 minutes
  3. Outer circle rotates; new pairs form
  4. Continue with new question each rotation

🔄 Socratic Seminar (Full Format)

Best for: Deep text analysis; student-driven inquiry

Preparation (Day Before):

  • Students read text with annotation focus
  • Generate 2-3 of their own discussion questions
  • Prepare textual evidence to support ideas

During Seminar (25-30 min):

  • Opening question from teacher (5 min)
  • Student-driven discussion (15-20 min)
  • Closing synthesis (5 min)

🔄 Philosophical Chairs

Best for: Polarizing topics; perspective-taking

  1. Statement posted: "[Provocative claim about text]"
  2. Students move to: AGREE / DISAGREE / UNSURE sides
  3. Volunteers from each side explain their position
  4. Students can switch sides if persuaded
  5. Final reflection: Did anyone change position? Why?

Scaffolding for EAL Students in Discussion

Challenge Scaffold/Solution
Processing time Provide questions 24 hours in advance; use Think-Pair-Share before whole-class
Vocabulary for discussion Post sentence starters and academic phrases; provide word bank
Participation anxiety Allow written contribution first; offer "pass" option; use smaller groups
Speed of conversation Appoint a "gatekeeper" who ensures everyone gets a turn; use talking stick/object
Abstract concepts Connect to concrete examples; use graphic organizers during discussion

The Socratic Seminar Toolkit

📋 Essential Sentence Starters for EAL Students:

Building on others: "I want to build on what [name] said..." / "Adding to [name]'s point..."

Respectfully disagreeing: "I see it differently because..." / "A different perspective is..."

Asking for clarification: "Could you explain what you mean by...?" / "Are you saying that...?"

Connecting to text: "On page [X], it says..." / "This connects to when [character]..."

Synthesizing: "What I'm hearing is..." / "The main ideas seem to be..."

Seminar Roles (Optional Structure)

🎯 Discussion Director

Keeps discussion focused; redirects if off-topic; ensures all questions are explored

📊 Evidence Tracker

Notes textual references; reminds speakers to cite specific pages/passages

🌉 Connector

Makes connections between ideas; links current discussion to earlier points

👂 Inclusion Facilitator

Watches for participation patterns; invites quieter voices; manages dominant voices

Post-Discussion Reflection

Learning happens in reflection. End every discussion with 5 minutes of written reflection:

  • One new idea: "I hadn't thought about..."
  • One changed mind: "I used to think... but now I think..."
  • One question: "I'm still wondering..."
  • One contribution: "I added to the discussion by..." / "I wish I had said..."
